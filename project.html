UK Cost of Living Crisis

Where did the pressure come from, who faced it, and has it eased?

GitHub LinkedIn

Aims

This project examines the UK cost of living crisis as a set of linked pressures rather than a single statistic. The central question is: how did prices, bills, and housing costs evolve relative to real pay, and how uneven were housing pressures across places? I operationalise the crisis via eight indicators covering consumer inflation, food prices, energy bills, fuel, housing costs, and real earnings.

Key idea Inflation falling back does not automatically mean the crisis has ended: price levels remain higher, energy bills may reset in steps, and housing markets are regional.

Data

All datasets are open access and documented in the accompanying notebook. Core sources include: ONS Consumer Price Inflation time series (CPIH and CPI components), ONS Average Weekly Earnings (real terms), ONS “Private rent and house prices, UK” chart downloads (regional and country rent inflation; rent vs house price inflation), Ofgem default tariff price cap levels, and DESNZ weekly road fuel prices. The notebook downloads raw CSV/XLSX, cleans them into tidy formats, saves cleaned CSVs, and writes JSON (records) for the site.

Tools & Methods

The pipeline is implemented in Python (requests, pandas, numpy). For replication, each dataset has a dedicated acquisition cell, a transparent cleaning step, and a final “export” step that writes to /data. Visualisations are implemented with Vega-Lite (Vega-Embed) for a static GitHub Pages site but with interactive features (tooltips, hover highlighting, and dropdown selection). Maps use a stable TopoJSON boundary file (ONS UK TopoJSON) and are joined to the data by official codes.

Conclusions

The evidence indicates a multi-channel crisis. Headline inflation provides the macro context, but food inflation and energy bills are key “felt” pressures. Real pay dynamics clarify whether households are regaining purchasing power. Finally, housing costs are spatial: rent inflation differs substantially across regions, implying uneven local affordability pressures. Further extensions could integrate distributional exposure (by income decile) and policy offsets (benefit uprating, energy support).

Evidence and visualisations (8)

1) Prices vs pay: CPIH and real earnings (indexed)

A core test of “cost of living” is whether pay keeps up with prices. This chart indexes CPIH and real earnings to a common baseline to show the relative path.

Data: ONS CPIH and ONS AWE real pay. File(s): data/vis1_prices_vs_pay.json.

2) Food inflation vs headline inflation

Food is a necessity and tends to be regressive in budget share. Comparing food inflation to headline CPIH helps isolate whether essentials were driving pressure.

Data: ONS CPI food component and CPIH. File(s): data/vis2_food_vs_headline.json.

3) Energy bills: Ofgem price cap step changes

Energy costs changed in discrete jumps around cap resets. The step pattern matters: households experience sudden bill increases rather than smooth inflation.

Data: Ofgem default tariff cap. File(s): data/vis3_energy_cap.json.

4) Transport: weekly road fuel prices

Fuel prices are volatile and influence both commuting costs and goods distribution. Weekly data highlights short-term variation that monthly CPI may smooth out.

Data: DESNZ weekly pump prices. File(s): data/vis4_fuel_weekly.json.

5) Housing pressure: rents vs house prices (annual inflation)

Housing is both a cost (rent) and an asset market (house prices). Divergence between rent inflation and house price inflation can signal distributional impacts across tenure.

Data: ONS private rent and house prices bulletin downloads. File(s): data/vis5_rent_vs_house.json.

6) Map: rent inflation across English regions (latest)

The crisis is spatial. This choropleth maps the latest regional rent inflation, revealing where affordability pressures are intensifying most rapidly. Hover to see exact values.

Data: ONS regional rent inflation. Boundaries: ONS UK TopoJSON regions layer. File(s): data/vis6_rent_map_regions.json.

7) Interactive trend: choose a region and track rent inflation over time

Cross-sections can be misleading if regions moved differently over the cycle. Use the dropdown to select a region and compare it to the England average.

Data: ONS regional rent inflation time series. File(s): data/vis7_rent_trend_regions.json.

8) Map: rent inflation across UK countries (latest available)

Devolution matters for housing and support policies. This map compares rent inflation in England, Wales, Scotland and Northern Ireland (latest available month for each).

Data: ONS UK country rent inflation. Boundaries: ONS UK TopoJSON countries layer. File(s): data/vis8_rent_map_countries.json.
