UK Cost of Living Crisis

Automated open data pipeline and eight interactive indicators

GitHub LinkedIn

Aims

The UK cost of living crisis is often discussed as a single phenomenon, but it is experienced through distinct channels: consumer prices, housing costs, energy bills, and transport. This project tracks those channels with a small, replicable set of indicators and asks: how have price pressures evolved, and to what extent has purchasing power (real wages) kept pace?

Data

All data are open-access and sourced from official producers: headline inflation (CPIH) and food inflation from ONS Consumer Price Inflation time series; owner occupiers’ housing costs (OOH) inflation from the ONS CPIH component; real earnings from ONS Average Weekly Earnings (real terms total pay); the Ofgem default tariff energy price cap levels; weekly road fuel pump prices from the Department for Energy Security and Net Zero; and rent/house price inflation series from the ONS “Private rent and house prices, UK” bulletin chart downloads. The pipeline downloads CSV/XLSX sources (and uses structured extraction only when needed), then regenerates the site-ready CSV and JSON files under /data.

Tools & Methods

The workflow is implemented in Python (pandas, numpy, requests). Each dataset is cleaned into a tidy format and saved twice: CSV for transparency and reuse, and JSON (records) for web visualisation. Real earnings are indexed to 2019=100 for interpretability. Visualisations use Vega-Lite (via vega-embed) and read local JSON files in /data, so the GitHub Pages site remains static but interactive (tooltips and responsive width).

Conclusions

The indicators show that “cost of living” is multi-dimensional: food and housing-related costs can remain salient even when headline inflation falls. Energy bills move in discrete steps around cap resets, while fuel prices are more volatile week-to-week. Comparing these costs with real earnings helps distinguish inflation relief from genuine living-standards recovery. Regional rent inflation varies materially across England, reinforcing that the crisis is not uniform. Extensions could add distributional analysis (e.g. by income decile) and policy variables (benefit uprating, energy support) to evaluate mitigation.

Visualisations (8)

1) Headline inflation (CPIH)

CPIH is the broadest commonly cited measure of consumer inflation. Tracking the annual rate provides the headline context for the crisis: when CPIH accelerated, households faced pervasive price increases across goods and services.

Source: ONS Consumer Price Inflation time series (CPIH annual rate). File: data/chart1_cpih_annual_rate.json.

2) Food inflation (CPI food)

Food is highly salient for living standards, especially for lower-income households. This chart isolates food inflation to show whether essentials were disproportionately driving cost pressures.

Source: ONS CPI food annual rate. File: data/chart2_food_inflation.json.

3) Owner occupiers’ housing costs (OOH)

Housing costs matter both for renters and owner occupiers. The OOH component in CPIH provides an additional lens on housing-related inflation pressures facing households.

Source: ONS CPIH OOH component annual rate. File: data/chart3_ooh_inflation.json.

4) Real wages (indexed, 2019 = 100)

Cost pressures become a “crisis” when incomes do not keep pace. Indexing real earnings to 2019=100 makes it easy to see whether households have recovered purchasing power relative to the pre-crisis baseline.

Source: ONS Average Weekly Earnings (real terms total pay). File: data/chart4_real_wages_index.json.

5) Energy price cap (typical annual bill)

Energy costs were a defining feature of the crisis. The Ofgem price cap illustrates discrete step-changes at cap resets and helps explain sharp shifts in household bills over time.

Source: Ofgem default tariff cap level. File: data/chart5_energy_price_cap.json.

6) Road fuel prices (weekly)

Transport costs affect commuting and goods distribution. Weekly fuel prices show higher-frequency volatility that can amplify household uncertainty, even when inflation aggregates smooth over short-term swings.

Source: DESNZ weekly road fuel prices. File: data/chart6_road_fuel_prices.json.

7) Regional rent inflation (latest month)

The crisis is uneven across places. This bar chart highlights variation in rent inflation across English regions, indicating different pressures in local housing markets.

Source: ONS private rent bulletin chart download. File: data/chart7_rent_inflation_regions.json.

8) Rent inflation vs house price inflation

Comparing rent and house price inflation helps distinguish pressures faced by renters versus dynamics in asset markets. Divergence between the two can suggest distributional consequences across tenure types.

Source: ONS private rent & house prices bulletin chart download. File: data/chart8_rent_vs_house_inflation.json.
