UK Cost of Living Crisis: Indicators Dashboard

Eight interactive visuals built from openly accessible UK official statistics. Data and code are designed for replication via a single Colab notebook.

Aims

The UK cost of living crisis is often discussed as a single phenomenon, but it is experienced through distinct channels: consumer prices, housing costs, energy bills, and transport. This project tracks those channels with a small, replicable set of indicators and asks: how have price pressures evolved, and to what extent has purchasing power (real wages) kept pace?

Data

All data are open-access and sourced from official producers: (1) headline inflation (CPIH, series L55O) and (2) food inflation (CPI, series D7G8) from ONS Consumer Price Inflation time series (MM23); (3) owner occupiers’ housing costs (OOH) inflation from the ONS CPIH component (series L5P7, MM23); (4) real earnings from ONS Average Weekly Earnings time series (real terms total pay, series A3WX, EMP); (5) the Ofgem default tariff energy price cap levels (XLSX); (6) weekly road fuel pump prices from the Department for Energy Security and Net Zero (CSV); (7–8) private rent and house price inflation series from the ONS “Private rent and house prices, UK” bulletin chart downloads (CSV).

The pipeline uses direct download links (CSV/XLSX) or, where necessary, structured extraction (e.g. HTML tables). Re-running the notebook refreshes the datasets and regenerates the files used by the website.

Tools & Methods

The workflow is implemented in Python (pandas, numpy, requests). Each source is downloaded, cleaned into a tidy format, and saved twice: CSV for transparency and reuse, and JSON (records) for web visualisation. Real earnings are indexed to 2019=100 to make the time path interpretable. Visualisations are built with Vega-Lite (via vega-embed) and read the local JSON files in /data, keeping the GitHub Pages site static while still interactive (tooltips, responsive width).

Conclusions

The indicators show that “cost of living” is multi-dimensional: food and housing-related costs can remain salient even when headline inflation falls. Energy bills move in discrete steps around cap resets, while fuel prices are more volatile week-to-week. Comparing these costs with real earnings helps distinguish inflation relief from genuine living-standards recovery. Regional rent inflation varies materially across England, reinforcing that the crisis is not uniform across places.

Extensions could add distributional analysis (e.g. by income decile), link rent pressures to housing supply measures, or incorporate policy variables (benefit uprating, energy support) to evaluate mitigation.
