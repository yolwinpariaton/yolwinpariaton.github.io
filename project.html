UK Cost of Living Crisis

Automated data pipeline and interactive visualisations

Aims

This project examines whether household resources kept pace with rising living costs in the UK, focusing on the inflation surge and the distribution of pressure across energy, food, transport, and housing. The aim is to connect a clear empirical question (“did pay keep up with prices?”) to transparent data sources and reproducible outputs.

Data

Data are sourced from open UK statistics (ONS) and public administrative releases. The workflow is replicable: cleaned CSV and JSON outputs are stored in /data. Visualisations use the JSON outputs directly.

Tools & Methods

Data processing is performed in Python with automated downloads and cleaning steps. Visualisations are produced using Vega-Lite and embedded as interactive charts.

Conclusions

The indicators show a consistent purchasing-power squeeze during the inflation surge, with energy and housing contributing strongly to pressure on household budgets.

1) Prices vs pay: CPIH and real earnings (indexed)

A core test of “cost of living” is whether pay keeps up with prices. This chart indexes CPIH and real earnings to a common baseline to show the relative path.

Source: ONS CPIH and ONS AWE real pay. File(s): data/vis1_prices_vs_pay.json.

Note: The y-axis is zoomed (98–114) rather than starting at zero to make differences between prices and pay visible.

2) Food inflation vs headline inflation

Food is a high-salience component of household budgets. This chart compares food inflation with headline inflation over time.

Source: ONS CPI food component and CPIH. File(s): data/vis2_food_vs_headline.json.

3) Energy price cap (typical annual bill)

Energy bills were a major driver of the crisis. The energy price cap indicates typical annual costs faced by households.

Source: Ofgem (typical annual bill). File(s): data/vis3_energy_cap.json.

4) Weekly fuel prices

Transport costs rose sharply during the post-pandemic recovery and global energy shock. Weekly fuel prices show the timing of these peaks.

Source: UK weekly fuel price series. File(s): data/vis4_fuel_weekly.json.

5) Rent vs house price inflation

Housing costs are persistent and unevenly distributed. This chart compares rent inflation with house price inflation.

Source: ONS private rents and house prices. File(s): data/vis5_rent_vs_house.json.

6) Map: Rent inflation across English regions

The crisis was not uniform across space. This choropleth highlights regional variation in rent inflation.

Source: ONS regional rents. File(s): data/vis6_rent_map_regions.json.

7) Rent inflation over time (interactive)

Use the selector to examine how rent inflation evolved across regions compared with England overall.

Source: ONS regional rent inflation. File(s): data/vis7_rent_trend_regions.json.

8) Map: Rent inflation across UK countries

This map compares rent inflation across the four nations of the UK.

Source: ONS country-level rent inflation. File(s): data/vis8_rent_map_countries.json.
